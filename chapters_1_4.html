AUTHOR'S NOTE

"They promise freedom, but they themselves are slaves of sin and corruption."

— 2 Peter 2:19

Have you heard of the Liberal Paradox? It's sometimes called Sen's Paradox, after Amartya Sen's article in the Journal of Political Economy, 1970, titled "The Impossibility of a Paretian Liberal."

Suppose you are looking forward to reading this book. You know you'll enjoy it. You think there's nothing wrong with books like these and it's healthy and normal to read them. Following Sen, let's call you Lewd.

Or suppose you think that books like these are indecent, unhealthy, abnormal, and should be burned unread. Again, following Sen, let's call you Prude.

Now suppose that Lewd would prefer convincing Prude to read it even more than reading it herself. She thinks it'll open his horizons and will help him to live a more enjoyable life. That's the kind of girl Lewd is.

As to Prude, he thinks it's simply wrong to enjoy reading such books. So if the book isn't burned, and if someone does read it, he'd prefer to read it himself rather than let Lewd read it. That's the kind of guy Prude is.

Who should read it? It's a conundrum, right?

A government worker to the rescue. Let's call her Shrewd. Being a part of the government, she has real power and can make things happen. As any government employee, she is a good person and makes her decisions carefully. She wants to make the best decision in the best interest of people, of course. Should Shrewd make Lewd read this book, make Prude read it, or burn it unread?

Shrewd knows her constituents and takes their wishes into account, trying to satisfy them as best as possible. She ranks "Lewd reads" higher than "Burn it" because Lewd wanted to read the book, and ranks "Burn it" higher than "Prude reads" because Prude thinks it's an indecent book and should be burned rather than read. Therefore "Lewd reads" is ranked higher than "Prude reads," and Shrewd's final judgement is for Lewd to read the book.

So what's the problem?

The problem is that both Prude and Lewd regard this outcome as worse than "Prude reads." Lewd wanted Prude to read it more than reading it herself, remember? And Prude preferred to read it himself rather than let Lewd enjoy it. Both got the outcome neither wanted.

This is just one example, but Sen proved in general that no social decision rule can simultaneously respect individual liberty and produce Pareto-efficient outcomes. In plain language: even a perfectly fair government, with perfect information and the best intentions, will make choices that leave everyone worse off than if they'd been left alone.

Three lessons. One: talk to each other about your preferences — be open, be sincere. Two: don't count on the government, or anybody else, to decide for you, because even with expertise and goodwill, they will screw it up. Three — the most important lesson of all — whether you are Lewd, or Prude, or Shrewd: read this book.

You may — no, you will — learn something you didn't know before. Something important. And who knows, upon reflection, you might even change your views on some familiar and universally accepted social constructs that reduce our freedom in the name of fairness. But above all — enjoy it.

It's not a very safe neighborhood. I check the peephole before opening. The hallway of a senior citizens' building at six-thirty in the morning: fluorescent lights buzzing, linoleum floor, the smell of instant coffee and medication. Mrs. Koval's door is closed. Mr. Petrenko's door is closed. The elevator is at the far end.

Everything is normal. Everything is quiet.

I lock my door, walk to the elevator, and press the button. While I wait, I think about the word ROMUS and the strobe and the dead phone, and then I stop thinking about it, because I have an exam, and an A, and parents who are counting on me.

I am nineteen years old. I am a virgin. I have $217 in my bank account. In approximately four hours, in Professor Bose's office — and no, I have no idea if he is by any chance a relative of Bose of Bose-Einstein — two FBI agents will interrupt my statistics exam and arrest me, and everything I believe about the shape of my life will turn out to be wrong.

But I don’t know that yet. Right now I’m riding an elevator in a senior citizens’ building in New Jersey, holding a dead phone and a girl’s notebook, and the crimson dream is already fading, and I cannot remember what it felt like to fly. Was there a call I am supposed to answer?

✶   ✶   ✶

CHAPTER FOUR: ROOMMATE

14 Days Before — Morning

"You will suffer the shame of going naked, because I will take revenge."

— Micah 1:8

✶   ✶   ✶

Strange that I have never visited Jezebel though she's invited me many times. I was always too busy for social calls, but now I have to return her notes before the exam. Her apartment on Hamilton Avenue is seven blocks from my building. Seven blocks I have walked a hundred times — past the laundromat with the hand-lettered sign that still says OPEN 24 HRS even though it closes at ten, past the bodega where the cat sleeps on the lottery machine, past the parking lot where someone has been slowly disassembling a Honda Civic for the better part of a semester.

I am carrying Jezebel's notebook and my dead phone.

I am two blocks from her building when I hear it — a sharp crack above me, like a branch snapping, except there are no trees on this stretch of Easton Avenue. Something strikes the sidewalk a foot to my left and explodes. Concrete chips sting my ankle. I stumble sideways into the gutter, and my knee hits the curb, and my hands go out to catch me, and they land in a puddle of something dark and oily that the city has not bothered to clean up.

I stay down for a second, breathing. Then I look at the sidewalk. A stone — smooth, gray, the size of a man's fist — sits in a fresh white divot in the concrete, still rocking slightly. If it had hit my head, I would not be looking at anything.

I look up at the roof of the building beside me — a three-story walk-up with a fire escape and a satellite dish. A figure stands at the edge. Long dark coat, collar turned up. He is perfectly still. He does not step back when I see him. He looks at me the way you look at something you are deciding whether to keep.

He raises one arm and points — not at me, but past me, in the direction I came from. What does he mean? What does he want? We hold eye contact for three seconds, four, five. Then he turns and walks away. Not quickly. The walk of a man who has done what he came to do.

I get up. My knee is scraped through my khaki pants. My palms are black with gutter water. The left side of my blue shirt is soaked from hip to shoulder. I look like I've been mugged, which, in a way, I have, except nothing was taken. Only delivered.

I pick up Jezebel's notebook. The cover is wet but the pages inside are dry. I pick up the stone. It is warm. Not from the air — the morning is cool. The stone itself is warm, as if someone held it for a long time, or as if the stone has its own heat.

I put it in my pocket and keep walking.

✶   ✶   ✶

Jezebel opens the door and her face changes immediately.

"What happened to you?"

"I fell."

"You fell." She looks at my soaked shirt, my filthy hands, the mud on my knees. "Where? Into what?"

"The gutter. Something — I don't know. Something fell off a roof and I jumped and hit the curb." I hold out her notebook. "Your notes are fine."

"I don't care about the notes. Come inside." She pulls me in by the elbow. "You're dripping on the floor."

I have never been inside Jezebel's apartment. We have always met on campus, or at my place, or at her front door — where last night she handed me the notebook in her nightgown and I walked home in the dark thinking about probability and crimson hair. Now I am standing in her hallway dripping gutter water onto the linoleum, and the apartment is small and warm, and it smells like cinnamon tea and something herbal I can't place.

"I know. I'm sorry."

"Stop apologizing and take off your shirt."

I hesitate.

"Gil, you're covered in street water. Give me your shirt and your pants. I'll throw them in the wash. You can shower and put something on."

She is already moving toward the closet. She comes back holding a robe — not a bathrobe, a woman's robe, the kind that ties at the waist. It is pale blue with small white flowers along the hem.

"This is all I have that might fit you."

"That will not fit me."

"It will cover you. Go shower. Bathroom is down the hall, door on the left."

I take the robe and my dirty clothes and walk down the short hallway. Two doors face each other — one on the left, one on the right. Left, she said. I open the left door.

Steam. A small bathroom — sink and mirror on the left, toilet on the right, bathtub straight ahead with the curtain pulled to one side. And standing in front of the bathtub, a woman. Arms raised, holding a towel behind her back, in the process of drying herself. Completely naked.

She is almost as tall as I am — six feet, maybe an inch more — with dark hair that falls wet past her shoulders. Traces of water on her face, her collarbone, the long line of her stomach. She is looking directly at me. She is not moving. I am not moving. The floor and ceiling are not moving. The hallway behind me has ceased to exist. Time has rearranged itself so that there is only this small humid room and the fact that I am standing in it holding a flowered robe while a woman I have never met stands before me without a single thing between her body and my eyes.

I see all of her at once, the way you see a landscape when lightning strikes — every detail illuminated simultaneously, none of it processed.

She is not embarrassed. She is not afraid. She looks at me with mild curiosity, as if a filthy stranger in the bathroom doorway is the kind of thing that happens on Tuesdays and can be handled between the shampoo and the conditioner.

"Who are you?" she asks.

"I'm sorry," I say. My voice does something unfortunate on the second syllable. "I'm — Jezebel said — the door — I'm Gil."

"Gil." She lowers her arms and wraps the towel around her hips. Her bottom half is covered. Her top half is not. She extends her hand. "Acindina."

I should shake it. This is what people do when introduced. But my right hand is gripping the robe like it is load-bearing structure, and my left hand is black with gutter filth, and I cannot make either of them do anything useful.

"You're dirty," she observes.

"I fell. Outside. In the — yes."

"The shower is yours." She steps past me — close enough that I can feel the heat still coming off her skin — and walks down the hall to her room. The door closes. I stand in the bathroom holding the flowered robe, alone with the steam and the smell of eucalyptus and the realization that my brain has recorded everything I just saw with the fidelity of a high-resolution camera and will never, under any circumstances, delete the file.

✶   ✶   ✶

I shower quickly. The hot water stings the scrape on my knee and turns the gutter grime brown before it swirls down the drain. I dry myself with the only towel available — hers, still damp, still carrying her scent, which does not help my situation — and look at the robe.

Jezebel is five foot three. I am six one. The robe was designed for someone of Jezebel's general proportions and has no provisions for someone of mine. I put it on. It covers me from shoulders to mid-thigh, which would be adequate if I were not also in possession of the physiological response that nineteen years of virginity and the preceding ninety seconds have produced.

I tie the belt as tightly as it will go. I check the mirror. My neck is exposed. My forearms are exposed. My legs, from mid-thigh down, are exposed. The robe's floral pattern does nothing to project authority. I look like a man who lost a bet with serious consequences.

I open the bathroom door and take small, careful steps back toward the main room. Knees together. Small steps. The Stone Guest making his entrance.

Jezebel and Acindina are setting the table. They both look at me. Jezebel presses her lips together. Acindina tilts her head.

"It suits you," Acindina says. She has dressed, but the word "dressed" is doing heavy lifting. A gray skirt short enough to qualify as a lemma, a pink blouse that conceals the way a window conceals. She sits down at the table.

The table is glass.

I realize this fact with an intensity I have never previously brought to the subject of furniture. The table is glass, and I am about to sit down at it wearing a robe that ends above my knees, and my body is doing something I cannot control and cannot conceal, and if I sit at this table, the glass will hide nothing.

"Coffee?" Jezebel asks. She is already pouring. She is enjoying this. She is my oldest friend and apparently she is enjoying this.

I sit down very carefully. I join my knees together. I place my hands flat on the glass and focus on the texture of the surface — cool, smooth, absolutely transparent. I do not look down. I think about polar bears. I think about Bose-Einstein condensates. I think about the stone in my pocket, which is still warm, which someone threw at my head forty minutes ago, which should be the thing I am thinking about instead of the woman sitting across from me.

"Jezebel says you're a student in her class," Acindina says.

"Yes."

"What do you study?"

"Statistics. I have an exam this morning."

She looks at me — not at my face, lower, at the general area of the glass table I am trying not to think about — and then back up. "You seem tense for a man who's only worried about an exam."

I take a sip of coffee and try to shift my weight without moving my knees apart. Under the glass table, the robe has done what robes do when you sit down. It has ridden up. I am fighting a war on two fronts — keeping my knees together and keeping the hem down — and losing both.

"You're very quiet," Acindina says.

"I'm thinking about the exam."

"No, you're not."

Jezebel makes a sound into her coffee that she converts, unconvincingly, into a cough.

✶   ✶   ✶

"Something happened on the way here," I say, because I need to talk about something other than what is happening under this table. "On Easton, two blocks from here. Someone threw a stone at me from a rooftop."

Both women stop.

"What kind of stone?" Acindina asks. Her voice has changed. Not the pitch — the temperature.

"A big one. Size of a fist. It hit the sidewalk a foot away from me. Would have cracked my skull."

"Did you see who threw it?"

"A man. On the roof. Long dark coat. He stood there and looked at me, then walked away."

“How tall was he? What was he wearing? Which building?”

The specificity of her questions is strange. Jezebel asks, "Are you okay?" — reaching across the table to touch my arm, which nearly undoes my entire structural arrangement. But Acindina is asking for clothes. Coordinates. Direction. Details.

"Taller than me. Long coat. The walk-up on Easton, near the laundromat. Three stories. Fire escape on the left side."

Acindina nods slowly. She is looking at the table, but she is not seeing the table. She is somewhere else — processing something that has nothing to do with coffee or statistics or the man sitting across from her in a floral robe.

"Do you still have it?"

"The stone?"

"Yes."

I reach into the pocket of my pants, which Jezebel has draped over a chair near the washing machine. The stone is still warm. I set it on the glass table. It sits there between the coffee cups, gray and smooth and wrong — a piece of the street that has followed me indoors.

Acindina reaches for it, then stops. She does not touch it. But she lowers her head slightly — and I could swear she inhales through her nose, quickly, as if testing the air above the stone. Then she sits back.

"You guys should not walk to campus alone," she says.

"We'll be fine."

"No. You won't." She looks at Jezebel. Something passes between them — not a conversation, exactly, but an exchange. Jezebel frowns. Acindina holds her gaze. Jezebel looks away first.

"I have an umbrella," Acindina says. "I'm coming with you."

"That's not necessary."

"I wasn't asking."

"It's a sunny day. Why would we walk under an umbrella?"

"Because someone just threw a rock at your head from a roof, and an umbrella is better than nothing."

She has a point. I hate that she has a point. I also hate that the shift from flirtation to command has not diminished my problem under the table — if anything, the authority in her voice has made it worse, which raises theological questions about my wiring that I do not have time to explore.

Jezebel checks the washing machine. "Done. Still damp, but wearable."

I have never been so grateful for damp clothing in my life. I take my pants and shirt to the bathroom, lock the door, and change out of the robe with the reverence of a man being released from a beautiful prison. The shirt clings in places and the pants are warm from the dryer, and I look like someone who got caught in a brief rain and lied about it, but I look like a human being, which is more than I could say five minutes ago.

When I come out, Acindina is waiting by the door, holding an enormous black umbrella — the kind meant for a golf cart or a small country — with BUY DOMESTIC BEER printed in white letters along the top.

"Ready?" she says.

"No," I say. "But let's go."
