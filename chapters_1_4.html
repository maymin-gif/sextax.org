“Do not give your strength to women, or your ways to that which destroys kings.” — Proverbs 31:3

AUTHOR’S NOTE

“They promise freedom, but they themselves are slaves of sin and corruption.”

— 2 Peter 2:19

Have you heard of the Liberal Paradox? It’s sometimes called Sen’s Paradox, after Amartya Sen’s 1970 paper in the Journal of Political Economy, “The Impossibility of a Paretian Liberal.”

Suppose you are looking forward to reading this book. Yes, it’s hot and heavy, but you know you’ll enjoy it. You think there’s nothing wrong with books like these and it’s healthy and normal for adults to read them. Following Sen, let’s call you Lewd.

Or suppose you think that books like these are indecent, unhealthy, abnormal, and should be burned unread. Again, following Sen, let’s call you Prude.

And yes — this book is, quite literally, one of those books. Lewd would press it into your hands. Prude would light the match.

Now suppose Lewd would rather convince Prude to read it than read it herself. She thinks it’ll broaden his horizons and help him live a more enjoyable life. That’s the kind of generous girl Lewd is. (Let’s write “>” for “prefers to.”)

Lewd’s preferences: Prude Reads > Lewd Reads > Burn the Book.

As for Prude, he thinks it’s simply wrong to enjoy reading such books. But if the book isn’t to be burned, he’d rather read it himself than let Lewd read it. That’s the kind of protective guy Prude is.

Prude’s preferences: Burn the Book > Prude Reads > Lewd Reads.

Who should read it? It’s a conundrum, right?

A government worker to the rescue. Let’s call her Shrewd. Being part of the government, she has real power and can make things happen. And as any government employee, she is a good person and makes her decisions carefully. She wants the right decision, in the best interest of the people, of course. Should Shrewd make Lewd read this book, make Prude read it, or burn it unread?

Shrewd is a reasonable person. She respects individual freedom: when a choice concerns only one person, that person’s own preference should decide. So:

— Whose opinion matters for “Lewd Reads” vs. “Burn the Book”? Lewd’s — it’s her book, her time, her eyes. Lewd wants to read it. So Shrewd ranks Lewd Reads > Burn the Book.

— Whose opinion matters for “Burn the Book” vs. “Prude Reads”? Prude’s — nobody should force him to read what he finds indecent. Prude would rather see it burned. So Shrewd ranks Burn the Book > Prude Reads.

Put them together, and Shrewd’s judgment is: Lewd Reads > Burn the Book > Prude Reads. Therefore Lewd reads the book.

So what’s the problem?

The problem is that both Prude and Lewd regard this outcome as worse than “Prude reads.” Lewd wanted Prude to read it more than she wanted to read it herself, remember? And Prude preferred to read it himself rather than let Lewd enjoy it. Both got the outcome neither wanted.

This is just one example, and Shrewd is just one face of it. Sen proved the result in general: no social decision rule — government, market, vote, algorithm — can simultaneously respect individual liberty and produce Pareto-efficient outcomes. (“Pareto-efficient” means that if everyone prefers A to B, then society ranks A above B — the minimum bar of honoring unanimous preferences.) In plain language: even a perfectly fair government, with perfect information and the best intentions, will make choices that leave everyone worse off than if they’d been left alone.

Three lessons. One: talk to each other about your preferences — be open, be sincere. Two: don’t count on the government, or anybody else, to decide for you, because even with expertise and goodwill, they will screw it up. Three — the most important lesson of all — whether you are Lewd, or Prude, or Shrewd: read this book.

You may — no, you will — learn something you didn’t know before. Something important. And who knows, upon reflection, you might even change your views on some rules we accept without thinking — that trade our freedom for someone else’s idea of fairness. But above all — enjoy it.

Do not remember the iniquities of our ancestors, but at this time remember your power and your name. For you are the Lord our God, and we will praise you. It is for this reason you have put the fear of you in our hearts.”

Then Dad says, “No sex, don’t cheat, be a good Jew.” Mom adds, “No hanky-panky, Gilochka, Gilochka, amen.” And I yell — as I have for years — “I got all A’s!” Dad smiles, which he does rarely, and that’s my reward. Mom is harder to read. She manages the candles and the dinner and the hand-washing, but she doesn’t have Dad’s intensity, and once, about a year ago, I caught a trace of a rolling eye when the command “no sex” was issued.

My father is a nurse specializing in sexually transmitted diseases. The horrors of his job explain his insistence. I don’t know if he’s happy in his work, but he never complains. One thing I’m fairly sure of: you can’t get rich treating STDs. My father hasn’t.

Sometimes, late at night, I’d find him sitting in the kitchen with the lights off, not reading, not doing anything, just sitting with his hands on the table like a man waiting for something that stopped coming a long time ago. He’d see me and smile and say, “Can’t sleep either?” But the smile never reached his eyes at those hours, and his face looked like it belonged to someone who’d once been capable of something enormous and had carefully, deliberately, put it away.

My parents have struggled to save money for my tuition. The main source was my mother reading palms for neighborhood women. She’s good at it and in demand, but she doesn’t like doing it — she told me once that everybody asks her if something could be changed and she doesn’t have a good answer. She brings home the bacon, so to speak, in her Jewish household. We don’t qualify for any of the grants available to groups we don’t belong to. My stipend — which depends on all A’s — is the only financial relief my parents get.

I know what my roots are. I know what cards I’ve been dealt. I am a single child in a traditional Jewish family — Russian émigrés, both of them. They came over in the early nineties, part of the wave. They speak Russian to each other at home — quick, low conversations in the kitchen that stop when I walk in, or don’t stop because they’ve forgotten I’m there. I never learned it. They never taught me. My mother said once that they left Russia so I wouldn’t have to carry it, and my father said nothing, which meant he agreed. I love my family. I had a great childhood, I love the celebration of Peisach and the Shabbat dinners. I love my friends from Hebrew school. But I feel, despite a little sense of guilt, that this style of life is not for me, that it is too structured, too appropriate, and dare I say, too dull?

When we were about twelve, Jezebel and I read about Icarus and decided to imitate his father’s invention. I built the wings out of flying kites and attached them with tape to my shoulders. Jezebel was in charge of risk management and built a landing place from old blankets under the fire staircase in our building. I climbed the stairs to give it a try.

Unfortunately, I slipped and fell from the second floor. I broke both of my legs and had to stay in bed for several months. Jezebel, who foolishly tried to soften my fall with her own body, got away with some minor scratches. It happened so fast that I didn’t have time to get scared, but I replayed the fall in my mind many times later. I remember thinking, “Is that the end?” I realized how easy it was to disappear, get erased, become irrelevant, and as long as I am still around, I want to become relevant, to make my life count.

I don’t know yet what I want — only that I want my life to count. The things I learn will help me find that purpose. Certainly wouldn’t hurt, right?

I had the flu and missed a few lectures on statistics. I’ve never liked taking notes anyway — note-taking distracts me from thinking. So I asked Jezebel, who normally sits near me during lectures, to lend me her notes for the last night before the exam, and she readily gave them to me.

I read Jezebel’s notes for another hour.

Then it’s time to go. I get dressed in khaki pants, a blue button-down, and sneakers. I throw Jezebel’s notebook and my dead phone — maybe the campus tech center can revive it — into my backpack and head for the door.

It’s not a very safe neighborhood. I check the peephole before opening. The hallway at six-thirty in the morning: fluorescent lights buzzing, linoleum floor, the smell of instant coffee and medication. The elevator is at the far end.

Everything is normal. Everything is quiet.

I lock my door, walk to the elevator, and press the button. While I wait, I think about the word ROMUS and the strobe and the dead phone, and Jezebel’s crimson hair, and then I stop thinking about it, because I have an exam, and an A, and parents who are counting on me.

I am twenty years old. I am a virgin. I have $217 in my bank account. In approximately four hours, in Professor Bose’s office — and no, I don’t know if he’s related to Bose of Bose-Einstein — two FBI agents will interrupt my statistics exam and arrest me, and everything I believe about the shape of my life will turn out to be wrong.

But I don’t know that yet. Right now I’m riding an elevator in New Jersey with a dead phone and a girl’s notebook in my backpack, and the crimson dream is already fading, and I cannot remember what it felt like to fly. Was there a call I am supposed to answer?

Icarus fell from the sky above the coast of Crete on a morning in early spring, when the sea was calm and the light was the particular pale gold that the Aegean produces in the hours before the heat arrives.

CHAPTER FOUR: ROOMMATE

Later that morning

“For this I will lament and wail; I will go stripped and naked.”

— Micah 1:8

Strange that I have never visited Jezebel though she’s invited me many times. I was always too busy for social calls, but now I have to return her notes before the exam. Her apartment on Hamilton Avenue is seven blocks from my building. Seven blocks I have walked a hundred times — past the laundromat with the hand-lettered sign that still says OPEN 24 HRS even though it closes at ten, past the bodega where the cat sleeps on the lottery machine, past the parking lot where someone has been slowly disassembling a Honda Civic for the better part of a semester.

In my backpack: Jezebel’s notebook and my dead phone.

I am two blocks from her building when I hear it — a sharp crack above me, like a branch snapping, except there are no trees on this stretch of Easton Avenue. Something strikes the sidewalk a foot to my left and explodes. Concrete chips sting my ankle. I stumble sideways into the gutter, and my knee hits the curb, and my hands go out to catch me, and they land in a puddle of something dark and oily that the city has not bothered to clean up.

I stay down for a second, breathing. Then I look at the sidewalk. A stone — smooth, gray, the size of a man’s fist — sits in a fresh white divot in the concrete, still rocking slightly. If it had hit my head, I would not be looking at anything.

I look up at the roof of the building beside me — a three-story walk-up with a fire escape and a satellite dish. A figure stands at the edge. Long dark coat, collar turned up. He is perfectly still. He does not step back when I see him. He looks at me the way you look at something you are deciding whether to keep.

He raises one arm and points — not at me, but past me, in the direction I came from. What does he mean? What does he want? We hold eye contact for three seconds, four, five. Then he turns and walks away. Not quickly. The walk of a man who has done what he came to do.

I get up. My knee is scraped through my khaki pants. My palms are black with gutter water. The left side of my blue shirt is soaked from hip to shoulder. I look like I’ve been mugged, which, in a way, I have, except nothing was taken. Only delivered.

I check Jezebel’s notebook in my backpack. The cover is wet but the pages inside are dry. I pick up the stone. It is warm. Not from the air — the morning is cool. The stone itself is warm, as if someone held it for a long time, or as if the stone has its own heat.

I put it in my backpack and keep walking.

✶   ✶   ✶

Jezebel opens the door and her face changes immediately. Something flickers in her eyes — quick, unguarded — before she sees the mud and the soaked shirt and becomes my concerned friend again.

“What happened to you?”

“I fell.”

“You fell.” She looks at my soaked shirt, my filthy hands, the mud on my knees. “Where? Into what?”

“The gutter. Something — I don’t know. Something fell off a roof and I jumped and hit the curb.” I hold out her notebook. “Your notes are fine.”

“I don’t care about the notes. Come inside.” She pulls me in by the elbow. “You’re dripping on the floor.”

I have never been inside Jezebel’s apartment. We have always met on campus, or at my place, or at her front door — where last night she handed me the notebook in her nightgown and I walked home in the dark thinking about probability and crimson hair and something about the way she looked at me in the doorway that I still haven’t examined and don’t intend to. Now I am standing in her hallway dripping gutter water onto the linoleum, and the apartment is small and warm, and it smells like cinnamon tea and something herbal I can’t place.

“I know. I’m sorry.”

“Stop apologizing and take off your shirt.”

I hesitate.

“Gil, you’re covered in street water. Give me your shirt and your pants. I’ll throw them in the wash. You can shower and put something on.”

She is already moving toward the closet. She comes back holding a robe — not a bathrobe, a woman’s robe, the kind that ties at the waist. It is pale blue with small white flowers along the hem.

“This is all I have that might fit you.”

“That will not fit me.”

“It will cover you. Go shower. Bathroom is down the hall, door on the left.”

I take the robe and my dirty clothes and walk down the short hallway. Two doors face each other — one on the left, one on the right. Left, she said. I open the left door.

Steam. A small bathroom — sink and mirror on the left, toilet on the right, bathtub straight ahead with the curtain pulled to one side. And standing in front of the bathtub, a woman. Arms raised, holding a towel behind her back, in the process of drying herself. Completely naked.

She is almost as tall as I am — six feet, maybe an inch more — with dark hair that falls wet past her shoulders. Traces of water on her face, her collarbone, the long line of her stomach. She is looking directly at me. She is not moving. I am not moving. The floor and ceiling are not moving. The hallway behind me has ceased to exist. Time has rearranged itself so that there is only this small humid room and the fact that I am standing in it holding a flowered robe while a woman I have never met stands before me without a single thing between her body and my eyes.

I see all of her at once, the way you see a landscape when lightning strikes — every detail illuminated simultaneously, none of it processed.

She is not embarrassed. She is not afraid. She looks at me with mild curiosity, as if a filthy stranger in the bathroom doorway is the kind of thing that happens on Tuesdays and can be handled between the shampoo and the conditioner.

“Who are you?” she asks.

“I’m sorry,” I say. My voice comes out half an octave higher than normal. “I’m — Jezebel said — the door — I’m Gil.”

“Gil.” She lowers her arms and wraps the towel around her hips. Her bottom half is covered. Her top half is not. She extends her hand. “Acindina.”

I should shake it. This is what people do when introduced. But my right hand is gripping the robe like it is load-bearing structure, and my left hand is black with gutter filth, and I cannot make either of them do anything useful.

“You’re dirty,” she observes.

“I fell. Outside. In the — yes.”

“The shower is yours.” She steps past me — close enough that I can feel the heat still coming off her skin — and walks down the hall to her room. And here is what I am not able to comprehend: her body, walking away, forms a perfectly straight line — spine vertical, head level, shoulders square — and at the same time curves at every place a woman’s body is supposed to curve. Hips, waist, the small of her back. It is not a model’s walk or a dancer’s walk. It is something older than either — the walk of a woman whose body knows what her mind has never had to learn. The door closes. I stand in the bathroom holding the flowered robe, alone with the steam and the smell of eucalyptus and the realization that my brain has recorded everything I just saw with the fidelity of a high-resolution camera and will never, under any circumstances, delete the file.

✶   ✶   ✶

I shower quickly. The hot water stings the scrape on my knee and turns the gutter grime brown before it swirls down the drain. I dry myself with the only towel available — hers, still damp, still carrying her scent, which does not help my situation — and look at the robe.

Jezebel is five foot three. I am six one. The robe was designed for someone of Jezebel’s general proportions and has no provisions for someone of mine. I put the robe on. I cannot raise my arms above chest height without hearing a seam protest. Something tears faintly under the left armpit. It covers me from shoulders to mid-thigh, which would be adequate if I were not also in possession of the physiological response that twenty years of virginity and the preceding twenty seconds have produced.

I tie the belt as tightly as it will go. I check the mirror. I hate mirrors. First of all, it’s pointless — what am I checking? My looks? Nothing I can do about them. You don’t like it, turn away. Also, mirrors are a trap: once you start checking, you can’t stop. But right now, standing in a woman’s bathroom wearing a woman’s robe after seeing a woman I’ve never met with nothing on, I need a status report. My neck is exposed. My forearms are exposed. My legs, from mid-thigh down, are exposed. The robe’s floral pattern does nothing to project authority. I look like a man who lost a bet with serious consequences.

I open the bathroom door and take small, careful steps back toward the main room. Knees together. Small steps. The Stone Guest making his entrance.

Jezebel and Acindina are setting the table. They both look at me. Jezebel presses her lips together. Acindina tilts her head.

“It suits you,” Acindina says. She has dressed, but the word “dressed” is doing heavy lifting. A gray skirt short enough to qualify as a lemma, a pink blouse that conceals the way a window conceals. She sits down at the table.

The table is glass.

I realize this fact with an intensity I have never previously brought to the subject of furniture. The table is glass, and I am about to sit down at it wearing a robe that ends above my knees, and my body is doing something I cannot control and cannot conceal, and if I sit at this table, the glass will hide nothing.

“Coffee?” Jezebel asks. She is already pouring. She is enjoying this. She is my oldest friend and apparently she is enjoying this. She glances at me over the rim of her cup. It’s about time to recognize that she is much more than just a friend. She is hot. She’s always been hot. When I look into her eyes I see a place I will not be able to leave.

I sit down very carefully. I join my knees together. I place my hands flat on the glass and focus on the texture of the surface — cool, smooth, absolutely transparent. I do not look down. I think about polar bears. I think about Bose-Einstein condensates. I think about the stone in my backpack, which is still warm, which someone threw at my head forty minutes ago, which should be the thing I am thinking about instead of the woman sitting across from me.

“Jezebel says you are friends,” Acindina says.

“Yes.”

“What do you study?”

“Statistics. I have an exam this morning.”

She looks at me — not at my face, lower, at the general area of the glass table I am trying not to think about — and then back up. “You seem tense for a man who’s only worried about an exam.”

“And you? What do you do?”

“I’m a programmer.”

I take a sip of coffee and try to shift my weight without moving my knees apart. Under the glass table, the robe has done what robes do when you sit down. It has ridden up. I am fighting a war on two fronts — keeping my knees together and keeping the hem down — and losing both.

“You’re very quiet,” Acindina says.

“I’m thinking about the exam.”

“No, you’re not.”

Jezebel makes a sound into her coffee that she converts, unconvincingly, into a cough.

✶   ✶   ✶

“Something happened on the way here,” I say, because I need to talk about something other than what is happening under this table. “On Easton, two blocks from here. Someone threw a stone at me from a rooftop.”

Both women stop.

“What kind of stone?” Acindina asks. Her voice has changed. Not the pitch — the temperature.

“A big one. Size of a fist. It hit the sidewalk a foot away from me. Would have cracked my skull.”

“Did you see who threw it?”

“A man. On the roof. Long dark coat. He stood there and looked at me, then walked away.”

“How tall was he? What was he wearing? Which building?”

The specificity of her questions is strange. Jezebel asks, “Are you okay?” — reaching across the table to touch my arm, which nearly undoes my entire structural arrangement. But Acindina is asking for clothes. Coordinates. Direction. Details.

“Taller than me. Long coat. The walk-up on Easton, near the laundromat. Three stories. Fire escape on the left side.”

Acindina nods slowly. She is looking at the table, but she is not seeing the table. She is somewhere else — processing something that has nothing to do with coffee or statistics or the man sitting across from her in a floral robe.

“Do you still have it?”

“The stone?”

“Yes.”

I get the stone from my backpack. It is still warm. I set it on the glass table. It sits there between the coffee cups, gray and smooth and wrong — a piece of the street that has followed me indoors.

Acindina reaches for it, then stops. She does not touch it. But she lowers her head slightly — and I could swear she inhales through her nose, quickly, as if testing the air above the stone. Then she sits back.

“You guys should not walk to campus alone,” she says.

“We’ll be fine.”

“No. You won’t.” She looks at Jezebel. Something passes between them — not a conversation, exactly, but an exchange. Jezebel frowns. Acindina holds her gaze. Jezebel looks away first.

“I have an umbrella,” Acindina says. “I’m coming with you.”

“That’s not necessary.”

“I wasn’t asking.”

“It’s a sunny day. Why would we walk under an umbrella?”

“Because someone just threw a rock at your head from a roof, and an umbrella is better than nothing.”

She has a point. I hate that she has a point. I also hate that the shift from flirtation to command has not diminished my problem under the table — if anything, the authority in her voice has made it worse, which raises theological questions about my wiring that I do not have time to explore.

Jezebel checks the washing machine. “Done. Still damp, but wearable.”

I have never been so grateful for damp clothing in my life. I take my pants and shirt to the bathroom, lock the door, and change out of the robe with the reverence of a man being released from a beautiful prison. The shirt clings in places and the pants are warm from the dryer, and I look like someone who got caught in a brief rain and pretended he didn’t, but I look like a human being, which is more than I could say five minutes ago.

When I come out, Acindina is waiting by the door, holding an enormous black umbrella — the kind meant for a golf cart or a small country — with BUY DOMESTIC BEER printed in white letters along the top.

“Ready?” she says.

“No,” I say. “But let’s go.”
